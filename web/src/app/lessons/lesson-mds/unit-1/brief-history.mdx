## Creating Quantum Theory
Volcanic ash fills the air as lava spews down from the side of a volcano. You, a physicist, sit amidst the ash looking at the molten rock and wonder, "Hmm, how can I model the radiation emitted by this magma?" That is exactly what physicists were doing in the late 19th century. Well, maybe not exactly.

### Ultraviolet Catastrophe
Prior to 1900, physicists were trying to model something called black body radiation. Lava is a good example of a black body, as light and energy hits its surface, the black hue absorbs most, if not all, of the energy. Black body radiation is the resultant radiation (or light) then emitted by this black body. Black body radiation was a surprisingly difficult thing to model at the time. The best description, called the [Rayleigh-Jeans Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rayleigh%E2%80%93Jeans_law), predicted that the radiation emitted by a black body would increase as the temperature of the black body increased. While this was a good description at large wavelengths of light, at small wavelengths this model predicted an infinite amount of radiation to be emitted (this is often dramatically called the Ultraviolet Catastrophe)!

### The Silent Killer
In the year 1900, a discovery was made! Physicist Max Planck proposed the first model successfully able to model black body radiation. He showed that the thermal radiation was in equilibrium with a set of harmonic oscillations (essentially, think of a black body as a bunch of "springs" that absorb and then emit radiation). The shocking detail: these oscillators could *only* ever have an integer (or *discrete*) energy level. To us, this comes naturally now. Things like the Bohr model of an atom only have specific energy levels an electron can be in. But to physicists of this era: this was a silent killer, overturning a century of intuition that the energy of an particle was continuous. Max Planck would go on to call the energy of one of these oscillators *quantized*, and the lower energy level of a particle of light a **quantum**. *Ladies and gentlemen, we've made it!*

This wasn't the only amazing thing that came with this discovery, in the *same* paper, Max Planck stated that the energy level $E$ of one of these oscillators was proportional to its frequency $v$ by a factor of $h$, known now as the **Planck constant**.

The equation:
$$
E=hv
$$
is now called the **Planck relation**.

### Quantization of Light
Only five years later, in 1905, while studying the [photoelectric effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect), Albert Einstein would apply this notion of quanta to light, stating it also had discrete levels of energy. A familiar equation was proposed:
$$
E=hf
$$
Where $f$ is the frequency of a light particle!

### Quantization of Matter
The year is 1913, and young Neils Bohr would be asked about [Balmer's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balmer_series) for describing the emission spectrum of an atom. "As soon as I saw Balmer's formula, the whole thing was immediately clear to me." (NEEDS CITATION) Bohr would then go on to create a new model of the atom which had quantized electron orbits, what we previously mentioned, the aptly named Bohr model.

### Matter Is Waving?
Well, in 1924, Louis de Broglie would make a stunning breakthrough: just like light, matter also has waving properties. In fact, he came to this hypothesis based off of Einstein's proposal regarding the the [photoelectric effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect), and his hypothesis that a mass at rest is proportionally equivalent to its energy $E=m_0 c^2$. De Broglie stated that matter in motion thus appears to have a wavelength $\lambda$ inversely proportional to its momentum: 
$$
\lambda = \frac{h}{p}
$$
or, using $\hbar=\frac{h}{2\pi}$.
$$
p=\hbar f
$$
This equation would both explain Bohr's quantization of atomic orbits, and show that the waving of light was an effect of quantization.
