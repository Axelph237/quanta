# Bra-Ket Notation
Often, you will see the quantum wave equation $\Psi$ often shown surrounded by these $|...\rangle$ or these $\langle ... |$ symbols. So what are these? The aptly named "bra" $\langle ...|$ and "ket" $|...\rangle$ in short represent a quantum state! For the sake of simplicity, we will only focus on kets and their meaning in quantum algorithms.

The **ket** $|...\rangle$ represents a quantum state. You can think of it like a label describing the state of a <span className="define-qbit">qubit</span>.

{/* essentially represents a <span className="define-vector">vector</span> $\alpha$ such that $a=|a\rangle$. Essentially, these just represent the vector itself. For instance, if $\Psi = \frac1{\sqrt2} (|0\rangle + |1\rangle)$, then $|\Psi\rangle = \frac1{\sqrt2} \begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}$  */}

{/* The **bra** $\langle ...|$ (although largely unused in our lessons) represents the <span className="define-hconjugate">Hermitian conjugate</span> of our vector $a$ such that $a^\dagger=\langle a|$. Don't worry too much about these :) they aren't super necessary for most of the lessons. In fewer words, the **bra** represents the row vector of $\alpha$. Using the above example, $\langle\Psi| = \frac1{\sqrt2} \begin{bmatrix}1&1\end{bmatrix}$ */}

{/* The differences between row and column vectors is unimportant for the remainder of Quanta, so feel free to not think too deeply about it! */}

## In Quantum Computing
For our purposes in quantum computing, bras and kets are used to represent the possible states of a system (usually expressed as a <span className="define-binary-string">binary string</span>). Most of the time, we just use kets in our equations. For example:

$|0\rangle \rightarrow$ similar to classical $0$

$|1\rangle \rightarrow$ similar to classical $1$.

For multiple qubits $|01\rangle$ means the first qubit = $0$, and the second qubit = $1$.

Take the following state, for example:

$$
\Psi=|1010\rangle
$$
In the above example (from left to right), qubit one = $1$, qubit two = $0$, qubit three = $1$, and qubit four = $0$.
