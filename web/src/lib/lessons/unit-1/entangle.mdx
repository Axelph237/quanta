# Quantum Entanglement
**In essence, quantum entanglement means a system of qubits must be described together, not independently.** This is integral for quantum computing because it allows us to perform logical operations between multiple qubits. Let's build an intuition about quantum entanglement.
## Quantum Casino
Imagine you walk into the world's first "Quantum Casino". Inside you go to a green, carpeted table with a croupier sitting at the other end. The game? Die Match. The game is simple: the croupier secretly rolls a number of dice and seals a random amount of them in two separate boxes. They hand you one box for you to look in, and keep the other. You get one chance to tell the croupier what faces are on all the dice. All you know is this: the sum of all the dice, and the total number of dice rolled. Do you think you'd win?
### Game One: Two Dice
You think so, so you sit down and place your first bet. The croupier rolls two dice and passes you a box. Only one die peeks up at you, its face showing a four. You are told the sum is seven. Easy! You think. The other die must be a three!

### Game Two: Four Dice
You decide to stay for another game. Rolling four dice this time, the croupier passes you a box of two and tells you the sum is 18. Looking into your box, you see the numbers four and five. Think for a moment about what numbers could be in the other box. You know that they must total nine, but there are two dice! The first could be a three and the second a six, or maybe another set of four and five. It's impossible for you to fully know what faces the dice must have.

## Quit the Gambling
So how does this relate to entanglement? Let's look at the two games: in the first the die are entangled, but the same can't be said for the second game. Why is that? In the first game, the total state (the sum) allowed us to infer the second die on measuring the first. This is the same with entanglement. When the state of two quantum wave functions are entangled, their states cannot be explained independently of each other meaning that when one of the wave functions is collapsed (measured) the other wave function also collapses (is measured), **no matter their distance**.

<Callout title="Please Note!" icon={<SchrodingersCat className="icon" />}>
Even though the *measured* outcomes are correlated, this does not mean each wave function had a definite value before measurement. The result of entanglement is a system of multiple wave functions (or qubits) that must be described as a single combined state.

It is also important we note that no signal or communication is sent when one entangled wave function collapses. Entanglement only correlates measured outcomes. If you then wanted to communicate with someone who has the other part of the pair, that would still have to happen across a different channel!
</Callout>
### The Math
Stepping fully away from analogies, let's look at the math. An example entangled system state could be represented by 
$$
\Psi=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|10\rangle+|01\rangle)
$$
Where $|q_0\rangle |q_1\rangle = |\alpha \beta\rangle$ is the order of qubits in a state.
A measuring of $q_0$ as $|1\rangle$ would tell us that $q_1$ must certainly be $|0\rangle$, and the same logic follows for measuring $|0\rangle$ in the $q_0$. If you attempted to factor the above wave function, you would find it impossible to divide it into two separate systems. That is how we know it is **entangled**. 

Here's another equation 
$$
\Psi=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|01\rangle+|00\rangle)
$$
it looks almost identical, with one key difference. Since both possible states contain $|0\rangle$ in the first $q_0$, it can be factored into $$\Psi=|0\rangle \otimes \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|1\rangle+|0\rangle)$$meaning this system is *not* entangled.
